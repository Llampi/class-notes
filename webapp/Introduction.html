The budget set

The budget set contains all the combinations of goods ---or bundles--- that the consumer can afford given the prices of the goods and her income.

Mathematically, the budget set includes all bundles that satisfy


The left-hand side of this expression is the cost of acquiring a specific bundle.
The right-hand side of the expression is just the income.
The inequality states that the person can only acquire bundles that cost less than the income she has!

Module 2.3: Preferences and Utility

Consumer Preferences

Consumer preferences tell us how the consumer would rank any two bundles of goods. We write


to mean that the consumer strictly prefers bundle to

We use ≽ to mean “weakly prefers” and the symbol ∼ to mean she is “indifferent”.
Properties of Preferences

We often impose restrictions on consumer preferences.
Three of them are of particular importance.

Completeness: The consumer can rank any pair of bundles. That is, for any two bundles (x_{1}, x_{2}) and (x’_{1}, x’_{2}), the consumer should be able to say whether (x_{1}, x_{2}) ≽ (x’_{1}, x’_{2}), (x’_{1}, x’_{2}) ≽ (x_{1}, x_{2}), , or both.
This restriction just eliminates the possibility that the consumer does not know what bundle she prefers.

Transitivity: For any three bundles (x_{1}, x_{2}), (x’_{1}, x’_{2}), , and (x’’_{1}, x’’_{2}) we have that (x_{1}, x_{2}) ≽ (x’_{1}, x’_{2}) and (x’_{1}, x’_{2}) ≽ (x’’_{1}, x’’{2}) implies (x_{1}, x_{2}) ≽ (x’’_{1}, x’’_{2}).

(If one of the initial preferences is strict, then (x_{1}, x_{2}) ≻ (x’_{1}, x’_{2}).)

Reflexibility: Each bundle is at least as good as itself. That is, (x_{1}, x_{2}) ≽ (x_{1}, x_{2}).


More on Restrictions on Preferences

Think for a little bit about the strength of these restrictions. Would you be able to rank any pair of COVID vaccines without the advice of an expert?

Though these conditions are strong, they are reasonable in a variety of contexts. They also constitute a good starting point!


Utility Function

In practice, it is sometimes hard to study choices directly from preferences. That is why we often use utility functions to describe them.

We say the utility function u(x_{1}, x_{2}) represents the preference ≽ if the next condition is true for any pair of bundles

(x_{1}, x_{2}) ≽ (x’_{1}, x’_{2}) if and only if u(x_{1}, x_{2}) ≥ u(x’_{1}, x’_{2}) .

Important Questions about Utility

Suppose that you find a utility function u(.) that represents some preferences ≽.

Is this representation unique?
No! Any increasing transformation of the initial utility function will represent the same preference.

Can any preference be represented by some utility function?
No! It has to be complete and transitive. Interestingly, these two conditions are necessary but not sufficient!

Widely used Utilities

The number of preferences and utility representations is unlimited. But three of them are quite popular.

Perfect Substitutes: u(x_{1}, x_{2}) = ax_{1} + bx_{2}
Perfect Complements: u(x_{1}, x_{2}) = min\{ax_{1},b_{2}\}
Cobb-Douglass: u(x_{1}, x_{2}) = x_{1}^{2} + x_{2}^{2}


Graphical Representation of the Utility

The graphical representation of the utility function takes place in a high-dimensional space. Nevertheless, in the case of two goods, we can describe the utility in a two-dimensional picture via indifference curves.

Later on, by overlapping indifference curves with the budget line, we will be able to describe choices!


Indifference Curve

An indifference curve is the set of all pairs (x_{1}, x_{2}) that give the same level of utility. That is, it describes a set of bundles that are equally preferred from the perspective of the consumer.

Two Important Concepts

There are two concepts relatedly to the utility function that are of particular importance in explaining choices:

The marginal utility and the marginal rate of substitution.

Marginal Utility

The marginal utility reflects the change in the utility level when we increase slightly the consumption of a given good.br

Since we have two goods in our model, we need to specify the marginal utility for each of them.

The marginal utility of good 1, MU_{1} , captures the change in the utility level when we increase slightly the consumption of good 1. Similarly, the marginal utility of good 2, MU_{2} , captures the change in the utility level when we increase slightly the consumption of good 2.


More on Marginal Utility

When the utility function is differentiable, then the marginal utilities of goods 1 and 2 take a simple form.

MU_{1}(x_{1}, x_{2}) = \alpha u(x_{1}, x_{2}) / \alpha x_{1}
MU_{2}(x_{1}, x_{2}) = \alpha u(x_{1}, x_{2}) / \alpha x_{2}




Marginal Rate of Substitution

The marginal rate of substitution, MRS, is the rate at which a consumer is willing to give up some amount of one good in exchange for another good while maintaining the same level of utility.


More of the Marginal Rate of Substitution

The marginal rate of substitution describes the slope of an indifference curve (in absolute value)! It relates to the marginal utilities in a simple way.

MRS(x_{1}, x_{2}) = MU_{1}(x_{1}, x_{2})/ MU_{2}(x_{1}, x_{2})
